25 authentic French expressions for everyday life in France — the ones French people actually say, not the ones your textbook taught you. Trained with the Echo Bounce method until they come out naturally.

The Echo Bounce method

For each expression below: listen to a native speaker, record yourself saying it, then play back and compare. 5 minutes a day. The loop is what makes it stick.

1
Listen
Hear the expression, the rhythm, the intonation.
2
Record
Say it out loud, in your own voice. On your phone.
3
Compare
Play yourself back. The gap closes. Repeat.

Sub-theme 1

The "Bonjour" routine

Five expressions you'll say every single day in France. The ritual of entering a shop, sitting at a café, asking for the check. Master these and the door to French everyday life opens.

  1. 01

    Bonjour !

    Hello! (entering any shop, with a smile — never optional)

  2. 02

    Vous êtes ouvert ?

    Are you open? (in front of a shop whose hours are unclear)

  3. 03

    Vous avez choisi ?

    Have you decided? (the server coming to your table)

  4. 04

    Je vous mets un pain au chocolat avec ?

    Would you like a pain au chocolat with that? (typical lunch offer)

  5. 05

    Je peux payer comment ?

    How can I pay? (the universal version — works everywhere)

Sub-theme 2

At the bakery, the market, the café

The heart of everyday life. What tourists don't dare to ask. What expats repeat 50 times before they dare to try.

  1. 06

    Je voudrais une baguette, pas trop cuite, s'il vous plaît.

    I'd like a baguette, not too well-done, please. (The most French sentence in the world.)

  2. 07

    Sur place ou à emporter ?

    For here or to go? (The question that stumps 90% of English speakers — every café, every bakery.)

  3. 08

    Vous me faites une remise si j'en prends trois ?

    Can I get a discount if I take three? (The ritual question at the market — the answer is often no, but the asking is the point.)

  4. 09

    C'est pour offrir.

    It's a gift. (For wine, flowers, chocolate — the shop assistant will wrap it for free.)

  5. 10

    Vous avez la carte ?

    Do you take card? (At the market or small shops where cards aren't always accepted.)

  6. 11

    Je peux goûter ?

    Can I taste? (The ritual gesture at the market. The vendor will cut a piece for you.)

  7. 12

    L'addition, s'il vous plaît ! On partage.

    The check, please! We're splitting. (At the end of a restaurant meal with friends.)

  8. 13

    Je vous dois combien ?

    How much do I owe you? (At the hairdresser, the mechanic, after a service where the price wasn't posted.)

Sub-theme 3

The French of neighbours and daily life

The art of living together — the famous savoir-vivre that francophiles dream of. The situations an English speaker living in France (or on a long stay) faces every week.

  1. 14

    Vous auriez l'heure, s'il vous plaît ?

    Do you have the time, please? (Polite conditional — the magic structure of polite French.)

  2. 15

    Je peux vous emprunter… ?

    Can I borrow…? (sugar, salt, a corkscrew, an umbrella — the polite debt.)

  3. 16

    Il y a quelqu'un ?

    Is anyone there? (Knocking at a door — intonation rises on "quelqu'un".)

  4. 17

    Ça vous dérange si… ?

    Do you mind if…? (I play some music, I smoke on the balcony, the dog barks tonight.)

  5. 18

    On se voit quand ?

    When do we meet? (The order is reversed compared to English — "when" comes first.)

  6. 19

    Amène les enfants !

    Bring the kids! (Amener a person/animal with you — not "apporter" for objects.)

  7. 20

    Tu restes manger ?

    Stay for dinner? (The most French invitation — spontaneous, generous, almost always sincere.)

Sub-theme 4

The "small word" that saves the conversation

What separates a "textbook" A2-B1 from a "natural" B2-C1. The invisible hinges, the linking words, the dressed-up pauses. Once you master them, everything changes about how natural your French sounds.

  1. 21

    Tu sais quoi ? / Vous savez quoi ?

    You know what? (To launch a piece of news, a scoop, a confidence. The listener knows something's coming.)

  2. 22

    En fait,…

    Actually,… (THE hinge of spoken French. Use it anywhere. The "so…" of French.)

  3. 23

    Du coup,…

    So,… (Informal consequence marker. The rhythm of real life.)

  4. 24

    enfin, …

    Well, I mean, … (The universal hesitation marker. Use it to nuance, to soften, to restart a thought. More mature than "genre".)

  5. 25

    Bref !

    Anyway! / Long story short! (The closing word. Use it to wrap up — you sound fluent, in control.)
